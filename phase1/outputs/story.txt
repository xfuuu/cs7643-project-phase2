**The Monkshood Masquerade**

The rain did not merely fall upon the Thorne estate; it besieged it. By ten o’clock, the silver-gray deluge had turned the Sussex lanes into rivers, effectively sealing the manor’s heavy oak doors against the world. Inside, the masquerade continued with a forced, brittle gaiety. Arthur Penhaligon, a man whose reputation for discretion was as polished as his evening shoes, stood by the grandfather clock in the corridor. Sir Alistair Thorne had been explicit in his invitation: *“Be outside my study at a quarter past ten, Penhaligon. I require a witness of impeccable character for a reckoning long overdue.”*

The clock chimed the quarter-hour. The study remained silent. 

Arthur knocked, received no answer, and pushed the door open. The room smelled of old paper, rain-heavy air, and something sharper—a faint, vegetal bitterness. Sir Alistair was slumped across his mahogany desk, his grotesque Harlequin mask pushed up onto his forehead, revealing eyes that stared at nothing.

Arthur did not panic. He secured the door and moved with practiced efficiency. On the desk sat a silver brandy flask, its cap unscrewed. A smudge of white, crystalline residue clung to the rim. Arthur’s gaze drifted to the floor, where a small scrap of green silk ribbon lay snagged on the sharp corner of the desk, as if torn away in a frantic retreat. Sir Alistair Thorne was dead, and the "reckoning" he had promised had taken a permanent form.

“Gather everyone in the drawing room,” Arthur told the butler ten minutes later. “The roads are out. No one leaves this house.”

The suspects were a tableau of 1920s anxiety. Lady Elspeth Thorne sat with icy detachment, her silk gown shimmering under the chandelier. Julian Thorne, the heir, was visibly perspiring, his green Robin Hood costume looking suddenly garish—Arthur noted immediately that a ribbon was missing from his sleeve. Dr. Percival Vance and his wife, Eleanor, stood by the hearth. The doctor looked clinical but pale, while Eleanor was a picture of poised concern, her hand resting supportively on her husband’s arm.

“Alistair has been murdered,” Arthur announced. The reactions were a chorus of gasps, save for Lady Elspeth, who merely tightened her grip on her fan.

Arthur began with Lady Elspeth. She claimed to have spent the last hour reading in the library. Arthur excused himself briefly to check. He found her wine glass on a side table, a distinct ring of red lipstick on the rim, but when he reached for the volume she claimed to be reading—a history of the East India Company—his fingers came away grey. The book was coated in a thick, undisturbed layer of dust. Elspeth’s alibi was a lie; she had been elsewhere.

Returning to the study to re-examine the scene, Arthur noticed a flicker of reflected light in the hearth. Beneath the charred logs lay a decorative hunting knife, its blade wiped clean but still damp. 

He summoned Julian Thorne to the ballroom. The young man was defensive, his eyes darting. “I was on the terrace,” Julian snapped, gesturing toward the French doors. “Getting some air. The mask was stifling.”

Arthur pointed to Julian’s sleeve. “You’re missing a green ribbon, Julian. I found one just like it snagged on your father’s desk. And I found the knife.”

“I didn't kill him!” Julian cried, his bravado collapsing. “I went in there to—to take the ledger. He was already slumped over! I thought he was asleep, but when I saw he wasn't breathing, I panicked. I stabbed him to make it look like a robbery gone wrong, but I couldn't go through with the rest. I threw the knife in the fire and ran.”

Arthur looked at Julian’s boots, then at the terrace doors. “The storm is a wall of water, Julian. If you’d been on the terrace, you’d be soaked to the bone. Your evening clothes are bone dry, and there are no footprints in the mud outside those doors. You weren't on the terrace. You were in that study.”

A search of Julian’s quarters later revealed a hidden ledger page detailing staggering gambling debts. Julian had a motive—the inheritance—and he had been at the scene. But Arthur was troubled. He returned to the body and performed a grim, closer inspection. He noted the knife wound; there was almost no blood. The heart had not been pumping when the blade entered. 

“Julian Thorne attempted to mask a crime that had already occurred,” Arthur murmured to himself. 

He turned his attention to Dr. Percival Vance. During a quiet interview in the guest wing, Arthur noticed the doctor’s hand trembling as he clutched his medical bag. When Arthur stepped forward, Vance tried to shove a crumpled slip of paper deep into the leather folds. 

“I’ll have that, Doctor,” Arthur said firmly.

It was a blackmail note in Alistair’s cruel, bold hand. It referenced a ‘fatal surgical error’ in London three years prior. Dr. Vance looked as though he might faint. “He was going to ruin me,” he whispered. “Tonight. At midnight. He invited you here to watch him strip me of my profession.”

Arthur looked at Eleanor Vance, who had appeared in the doorway. She was calm, her face a mask of loyal devotion. “My husband was with me,” she said smoothly. “Or in the washroom. He never left my thoughts.”

Arthur’s mind turned to the bitter scent in the study. He navigated the darkened corridors to the manor’s conservatory. Among the exotic ferns and shadowed blooms, he found the monkshood—*Aconitum*. In a nearby waste bin, he discovered a single suede glove, stained with crushed organic residue and damp soil.

The final piece of the puzzle fell into place in the entrance hall. As the guests sat in a hollow silence, Arthur checked the cloaks and bags. In Eleanor Vance’s beaded evening bag, nestled in the silk lining, was a single, vibrant purple petal of monkshood.

At 12:35 AM, Arthur gathered them all back in the study. The atmosphere was suffocating.

“Sir Alistair was killed three times tonight,” Arthur began, his voice echoing against the dark wood. “First, by a predator’s greed. Second, by a son’s desperation. And third, by a wife’s devotion.”

He held up the silver flask. “This contains Aconitine. A swift, cardiac poison. Julian Thorne found his father dead and, in a moment of madness, used a hunting knife on a corpse to stage a struggle. But the poison was already in the flask.”

Arthur turned to Eleanor Vance. He laid the stained glove and the purple petal on the desk. 

“You knew your husband couldn't survive the revelation Alistair planned for midnight,” Arthur said. “You saw the monkshood in the conservatory, and you knew exactly what it could do. You poisoned the flask during the masquerade, protected by your mask and the crowd. You did it to save your husband’s career and his life.”

The room was silent, save for the lash of rain against the glass. Eleanor Vance stood up, her gaze never wavering. She looked at her husband, who was weeping silently.

“Alistair Thorne was a monster who broke people for sport,” Eleanor said, her voice chillingly steady. “He was going to kill Percival’s spirit tonight. I simply ensured the monster died first. I would do it again.”

As the first grey light of dawn broke through the clouds, the floodwaters began to recede. The local constabulary, summoned as soon as the lines were clear, arrived to find a house of broken masks. Eleanor Vance was led away, her head held high, while Julian and Lady Elspeth were left to wither in the shadow of their own smaller shames. Arthur Penhaligon stepped out into the damp morning air, the "witness" to a reckoning far more absolute than Sir Alistair had ever intended.
